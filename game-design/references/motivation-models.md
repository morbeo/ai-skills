# Motivation Models Reference

## Self-Determination Theory (SDT)

Three innate psychological needs that drive intrinsic motivation:

| Need | In games | Design implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Autonomy** | Player feels in control of meaningful choices | Avoid busywork; make choices matter |
| **Competence** | Player feels capable and growing | Difficulty curve; feedback; mastery moments |
| **Relatedness** | Player feels connected to others / the world | Social systems; NPCs with depth; community |

SDT predicts that games satisfying all three create intrinsic motivation (play for its own sake).
Games satisfying only extrinsic motivation (rewards) see player churn when rewards stop.

## Fullerton's Player Types

From *Game Design Workshop* (Tracy Fullerton):

| Type | Motivation | Design for them |
|---|---|---|
| **Competitor** | Winning against others | Leaderboards, PvP, ranked modes |
| **Explorer** | Discovering the world | Hidden areas, lore, procedural variety |
| **Collector** | Completing sets | Achievements, galleries, inventory systems |
| **Achiever** | Mastering challenges | Skill-based progression, trophies |
| **Storyteller** | Experiencing narrative | Strong characters, meaningful choices |
| **Performer** | Expressing themselves | Cosmetics, emotes, creative tools |
| **Craftsman** | Building and optimizing | Base building, theorycrafting, customization |

Most players are mixes. Design for your primary audience without shutting out others.

## Quantic Foundry Model (Yee)

Data-driven from 400k+ players. Six motivational components in three clusters:

**Action cluster**
- *Destruction* — blow things up, combat power
- *Excitement* — fast-paced, intense experiences

**Social cluster**
- *Competition* — performing better than others
- *Community* — interacting with other players

**Mastery cluster**
- *Challenge* — difficult, demanding gameplay
- *Strategy* — careful thinking, planning

**Immersion cluster**
- *Story* — following characters and narrative
- *Fantasy* — becoming someone else
- *Design* — creative expression

**Achievement cluster**
- *Completion* — collecting, 100%-ing
- *Power* — becoming strong, dominant

**Discovery cluster**
- *Discovery* — finding hidden things, exploring

Use this to profile your target player and audit whether your mechanics serve their motivations.

## Flow (Csikszentmihalyi)

Flow = optimal experience = challenge matches skill exactly.

```
          High
           │         ╔════════╗
  Anxiety  │        ╔╝ FLOW  ╚╗
           │       ╔╝  ZONE   ╚╗
           │      ╔╝           ╚╗
  Boredom  └────────────────────── High
                      Skill
```

Design principle: **dynamic difficulty** — adjust challenge in response to player
performance to keep them in flow. Explicit (difficulty settings) or implicit
(rubber-banding, procedural scaling).

Signs a player left the flow zone:
- Anxiety: rage quits, complaints about unfairness, long pause before retrying
- Boredom: skipping dialogue, rushing, disengagement

## Tabletop Motivation Patterns

Physical games have distinct motivation patterns from digital:

**Social proof** — playing a physical game is a social signal. Aesthetics matter more than in digital games because the object lives on a shelf.

**Ritual** — setup/teardown is part of the experience. Lean into it or minimize it intentionally; never ignore it.

**Tactile agency** — touching pieces, shuffling cards, rolling dice satisfies differently than clicking. Design for the hand, not just the eye.

**Session arc** — tabletop players commit to a session. Design a satisfying arc within a single session (even for campaign games, each session needs its own arc).
